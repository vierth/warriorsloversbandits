The Water Margin

Outlaws of the Marsh

All Men are Brothers

This is one of the so called four classic novels of imperial China, along with the Three Kingdoms, Journey to the West, and the Dream of the read chamber. Unlike the Three Kingdoms, the water margin was written in vernacular Chinese and tells the story of how a group of bandits gathered at Liang Shan marsh.

They were lead by the bandit Song Jiang. The exploits that are described in the novel are almost entirely fictional and emerged as part of folk retellings of stories that were first told during the Song dynasty. This being said, Song Jiang himeself was a historical figure who did lead an uprising against the emperor Huizong of the Song.

There are multiple endings to the novel, depending on which version you read, which depend pretty heavily on the given editor's opinions about the message of the novel. In some verisons, the bandits end up defending the dynasty and are eventually pardoned. In other versions, they are executed for their treachery to the kingdom.

Shi Nai'an

The Water Margin is attributed to a man named Shi Nai'an, although it is not clear who actually wrote the novel (as we've mentioned on numerous occasions in this calss, early novels were not well regarded and it wasn ot importnat to establish firm authorhip.

Some people claim that Shi Nai'an was a teach of Luo Guanzhong and that Luo may have edited the water margin (though this is pretty unlikely).

Supposedly Shi lived from around 1296 until 1372, though we know basically nothing about him.

Song Dynasty

https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:China_-_Song_Dynasty_-_cs.svg#/media/File:China_11a.jpg

While it was written during the Ming dynasty (or possibly the late Yuan), the novel is set in the Song dynasty. The Song was one of the longer dynsaties and ran from 960 to 1279. It was established after the Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms which occured after the fall of the Tang dynasty.

Like the Han dynasty, the Song dynasty is divided into two periods. The first half was the NOrther Song, because the capital was in modern day Kaifeng (960-1127), and the Southern Song with its capital in modern day Hangzhou (1127-1279)

The song saw significant scientific and govermental innovatoins, with the invention of gunpowde, more extensive printing, and the invention of moveable type.

There was also lots of innovation in culture, with the development of newer literary genres

108 Bandits

36 Heavenly Spirits

72 Earthly Demons

This novel, unlike the others we have read, is centrally character driven. There are 108 bandits, and we get a greater or lesser amount of information about each one, depending on where they figure into the overall picture of the novel's ideas about prominence. The most important characters, which get most of the stage time are the so called 36 heavely spirits, while the rest of the bandits are earthly demons. As you know, the novel opens with them let out of the temple in which they are locked.

Not all of them get much screen time, but the novel begins with the adventures that lead them to Liang Shan marsh. In most cases, this shows what they end up doing to deserve exile.

Shuihu Zhuan and Military Subculture

Paul Jakov Smith introduces some importnat ideas about the water margin in his article on military subculture in the Northern Song dynasty. The first is that this novel is fairly unique in the range of characters it presents. because it is about bandits, it discusses people who lived all different layers of society, from the lowest of the low all the way up to the emperor himself.

He starts by focusing on arms instructors, and on page 365 he points out that "As Gao Qiu's first victim..."

The important point here is that there are arms instrcutures distributed and multiple levels in society, and they tend to be there to help local lords at the manorial elvel (the focus of much of the action in the novel).

There is an obsessive focus on martial arts among many of the characters and Smith argues on page 369 that Shajin perfectly embodies this...

Smith argues that the patronage networks we see in this novel place them on a border between officialdom (which they ostensibly serve) and outlaws (which they have a fraternal bond to).

The manorial organization of much of society plays an importnat role as well, as local aristocrats started to accumulate estates in the Song that required defense (thoguh scholars argue about how well the novel presents this system). On Page 375 smith asks "But would large landowneers..."
