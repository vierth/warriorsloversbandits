Martin Huang

This article focuses on the nature of authorship in late imperial Chinese fiction (or xiaoshuo, as huang is clear to keep distinct). But he opens his article with a focus on the practice of offering commentary (or xiaoshuo pingdian, the act of adding commentary and punctuations that aid readers in interpretation). When reading these works in the 17th century it was only natural that these were a central focus of reading practice, but many modern typeset editiosn completely dump them, which deprives us of much of the context making apparatus that contemporary readers enjoyed.

Another key issue Huang notes in trying to assess what authorship means in the Chinese xiaoshuo context is the complicated textual history of every book. What hapens when the book we have recieved has undergone signficant transformation from the days before the xiaoshuo itself was written (in terms of its source materias) through to later days when commentators edited the texts to better fit with their own ideas?

Huang hopes to reframe this discussino of authroship in light of the commentaries. He does place this in light of western theoretical discussions by discusing Barthes and Foucault, who both wrote very critical essays on authorship (Barthes wrote The Death of the Author, and Foucault wrote What is an Author). For Foucault "THe author-function is therefore characteristic of the mode of existence, circulation, and functioning of certain discourses within a society." It is something that only really becomes important when someone might need to be punished for what they wrote.

Huang links "authorship" to "authority" and connects this with very early ideas of about authorship found in confucian writting. Who has the authority to write a text, and in the earliest days it was the Shengren (sage). Confucius very famously said 述而不作. I transmit, I don't author.

Jin Shengtan, the author of the most important commentary on the Water margin etners into the conversation with precisely this idea: only the sages have the authority needed to author things, but this has not stopped many others from doing so. The result is a lot of very bad writings that should never have been written. it is also the reason why he imself cannot write but must stick to commenting on things.

The practical result of all of this is the notion of authorship in Chinese literature is not an easy one, and it is even more confused when we look at novels and their commentaries, in which "This blurring of the distinctinos between an author and a commentator was much much more common and signficant in the productino and consumption of traditional xiaoshuo commentaries" "'author' was often a figure carefully manipulated by the commentator to enhance the authority of his own interprtation of the work. Here, the author's control of the meaning of a text was seriously challenged."

Antholgoizing became very important aspect of late ming literary production, and an interesting move that some of these compilers made was that it was valid to offer an interpretation of a piece of literature that might differ from the original author's intent. This was necessary in some cases because works were created so far into the past that we can't actulaly recover intent in any meaningful way.

When we start thinking about novels, things become even more complicated, because of the absense of a fixed author (and as huang states "writeres were more inclined to consider themselves 'compilers' or 'editors' rather than real 'authros'") which in terun gave more space for commentators to influence the text.

The Water Margin offers a very interesting example of this, given the different versions of the text that exist (long versions ranging from 100 to 120 chapters) and short version around 70 chapters (Jin shengtan also added a preface to the novel which he attributed to Shi Nai'an, but which he wrote insmelf)

53: "One thing shared by Zhu Xi and Jin Shengtan..."

Importantly, these commented editions of xiaoshuo tended to become the definitive versions (the mao version of the sanguo, jin's water margin, and zhang zhupo's jinpingmei).

There is also an interesting promotion of the reader over the "author" that we see in these commentaries, noting the act of adding these commentaries creates a new text.

On page 61 Huang notes Zhang Zhupo's attitude: "if you read the Chin Ping Mei as a work of literature..."

we should hesitate to understand this as a readers response, as huang points out, because this more about policing the meaning of the text that saying that any reader's interpretation is equally valid.

Actual vs implied author is importnat. The historical author is not really that meaningful but the author as implied through a careful reading of the text is what matters.
