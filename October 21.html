Theaters of Desire

Let's spends some time talking about the things that Patricia Sieber brings up in the chapter I assigned from her book Theaters of desire, which focuses on commentaries written about The Story of the Western Wing, and waht this tells us about how the work was recieved at various points in history. Can anyone sum up the arguments she makes really quickly for me?

She begins by discussing Guo Moruo, an important intellectual thinker during the May 4th movement, who was interested in making sure the Story was not relegated to the dustbins of history because of some percieved erotic content. He wrote the preface to a vernacular edition of the drama to be published in Shanghai in the very early 20th century. He noted the evolution of depictions of love in the drama, away from "A sublim state of mind" to "erotic passion" as Sieber puts it (thogh we haven't gotten quite that far in the drama yet).

Guo took a Freudian approach, tying the sexual events in the novel with the author's own supposed sexual nature. Read bottom of first page "When we carefully read the XIxiang ji...."

Yet Guo's focus is on the author, wherease more contemporary commentators, who wanted to put their own spins on the novel, were less concerned about the author and more focused on the nature of the readers. "Their misgivings about 'obscenity' derived from the nera-universal dissemination of the play among all strata of the population from the Yuan period on."

Sieber points out the ubiquity of the performance of the play in both private and public venues, even as the music that backed the play fell out of style, it was readapted into other formats. I did see if I could find an origial performace of the play, but the only versions I could track down were southern-style Kunqu versions.

She points to Wang Jide's 1614 edition of the novel and Jin Shengtan's 1656 version, whcih he called the sixth book of genius. Interestingly, by calling it this he links it to other very important works (including the Water Margin, which he called the fifth book of Genius).

Their ideal readership was clear, but slightly different. On page 125, Sieber says "Both Wnag and Jin..."

So obscenty then becomes linked to "proper and imporpoer channels of circulation, rather than as a quality inherint in a writer, a musical piece, or a text."

There were several dozen editions of the play, some meant to be performed and others to be read.
