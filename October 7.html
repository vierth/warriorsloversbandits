Taking Historical Fiction Seriously

We've talked a lot in this class about how history can end up in fictional works, with both the Romance of the Three Kingdoms, and to a lesser extent, with the Journey to the West (focusing largely on how Journey may have rubbed some contemporaries the wrong way. But what if we step back for a moment and think about how these novels can act as an instructive medium, as serious vehichles for historical information in and of themselves.

Wills argues that "Mainstream academic historians should take historical novels seriously, as extremely influential and effective transmitters of historical understanding to large audiences and as manifestations of a narrative consiousness that is and ought to be an important element in the work of many writers of nonfiction history."

Wills points out that he first picked this idea up when reading robert Van Guliks translates of the Judge Dee mystery stories. These were novels based on court cases in which a maginstrate, one judge dee was in charge of solving a mystory.

In these, Wills saw an expression of life in China in the 15th/16th centuries that was valuable for historical understanding that "includ[es] some vivid portraits of beggars, peddlers, and others whose lives we know in an abstract way from the histrocial recor, characters who frequently appear in Chinese fiction, but who hardly ever can be portrayed as individuals in nonfiction history."

This is not just useful for cultural historians, they are also an importnat source for the general populace taht is not able to travel widely to see monuments and histories (note that this article was written in the 1980s and things ahve changed quite a bit).

Wills discusses the novel Shogun by James Clavell as a cite of historical discourse, even though, as wills puts it, there were some errors deeply embeddedin the structure of the novel. Regardless, it shows the psychology of another place/age.

Wills also brings in televisijon serios of Shogun as a broader popularization of the narratives within the novel.

Wills also mentiosn Robert Elegants Manchu, which introduces readers to a fictionalization of the Ming-Qing transition. He offers much less praise of this novel: "the dialogue is wooden, none of the characters is psychologically credible, and the imparting of information is not sustained by the reader's participation in the anlien protagonists progress toward understanding; the height of his appreciation of Chinese culture seems to be the perception that Chinese politics are unfathomably complex and subtle... But the novel made me think. Had I written it, I would not have given the Deng-zhou rembeillion 0f 1632 the importance that Elegant does in his narrative of the civil wars, but reading his made me want to go back and look again."

So this becomes useful as a counternarrative that forces better understandign of the period.

This is not a phenomenon limited to novels about Asia, as he points to Ivanhoe as an example of romanticizing the English Middle ages.

Roots is another exmaple of this. Has anyone seen roots? We watched it when I was in highschool, but it came out in the early 1980s.

This essay touches on some of the interesting moral issues historians face: "Those who read nonficiton history with fascination and even try to write it will find one important element in their attraction to history completely missing from the histroical novel: the pathos of teh search for what actually happened. For many of us, the shock of reality, the astonishment that we can know as much as we do about past events is a deeply human part of the appeal fo history." That this is missing from historical novels is perhaps the central difference, this lack of a moral centering of what actually happened. I'm not sure I myself buy this so much, but it is an interesting idea.

Still, as wills points out, the novel provides a glimpse of the general feel of certain eras in a way that histories cannot provide. So it becomes "what itmust have been like"

Wills points out that "The strengths of historical novels in comparison to other mass media are formidable". They can contain lots of information that can be read slowly and absorbed. And this for people who would not otherwise read a history ("especially one with footnotes")

"Henry Smith pointed out in his introduction to Learning from Shogun that many Japan schoalrs would respond to his titlte in the same way many architects responded to a book entitlted Learning from Lass fagos. In both cases, he suggested, a serious confrontatin with a phenomenon of vast popular appeal could help professionals sort out their basic priorities. If we can't learn to take histroical novels seriously, we ingore some of the most potent shapers of the general historical consciousness and we impoverish our own efforts to understand and explain the past"
