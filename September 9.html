How to Read the Sanguo

Today I had you read David Roy's essay on how to read the Sanguo, focused on Mao Zonggang's approach. The reason i did this is because it is importhat to understand how contemporary readers would have appraoched the San Guo, and one of the best ways of getting access to this is looking at what commentators had to say about it. Now if you recall, I mentioned previously that Mao and his father were responsible fro the most commonly read version of the novel, chaning poems, shortening the text, and so forth. But their readning of the novel had significant influence on its reception.

Mao Lun, the father has an interesting attitude towards creating the commentary, saying that he put it off until he went blind. What do you think is the significance of this? For me it is meant to convey that the creation of the commentary should not be viewed as a serious endeavor (and Mao Lun says as much directly). His commentary to the pipa ji was dictated to his son, Mao Zonggang

Mao Lun claimed that the Pipa ji was critical of a Wang Si, and contemporary commentators felt that this rethorical strategy was dumb. Of course it wasn't, there is literally no evidence that this is the case.

David Roy points out that the commentary to the Three Kingdoms was written in partial reaction to Jin Shengtan's essays on the Water Margin, which we will be reading later in the semester. But it is key to note that Mao argues the Three Kingdoms reaffirms conventional authority (the Water Margin subverts it). They emphasize the "moral rightness" of the text. Mao Zonggang definitely corresponded with Jin Shengtan, they both lived in the same city at the same time, but the Maos seem a little cheeky in how they regard Jin.

Both the Maos and Jin are happy to edit these extremely famous works they are writing about. Why do you think that is? Do you think it is a bit presumptuous to be editing it? We can do better? Note that the Maos claim they found an older edition and they are merely using the one that hews closest to the origijnal. This is a rhetorical strategy that is very common in this type of work. Meanwhile, Cao Cao has fully gained control of his territory, and Liu Biao, the person Liu Bei was working for in Chapter 34 from last time dies. His wife's family takes over and Liu Bei flees. The flight is succesful because Zhuge Liang lays a bunch of traps for Cao Cao.

The Battle of Red Cliffs

The main focus of the chapters we read for today is the Battle of Chi Bi or the battle of the red clifs, which is one of the most famous battles in the novel and in all of Chinese history. There is very little clear evidence of where this battle actually took place. It most likely took place somewhere along the Yangzi river but its been a matter of debate since the 300s exatly where this happened.
There are inscriptions in the side of the cliff at Chibi city in Hubei that suggest this is where the battle occured, dating from at least the Song dynasty. Note that this city was renamed to Red Cliff City in 1998 to link the location more tightly to the historical battle.
